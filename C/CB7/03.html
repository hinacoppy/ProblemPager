(CB7-03)   2015 - Position 3
Match Play. Double Match Point. How should Red play 22?
pips Red=126 White=114
(CorrectAnswer= 0/0, ErrorScore= 0)
I got this horribly wrong over the board by playing 13/11, 6/2*, 4/2 which is a blunder. With this move it is difficult to prime White and force him to crack on the other side of the board.

The key to the position is to make as many as possible of White’s sixes bad for him so that he is forced to break his prime. My play left only 16 (which my opponent rolled!) whereas 13/9, 7/5(2) (the best move) gives 63, 64 and 66.

It is necessary to spot the tactical opportunity afforded by White’s structure on his own side of the board in order to make the correct play. This is an instance where you don’t want to attack a lone checker – you need to let your opponent play with the hope that he will roll an awkward number. My play turned some of his worst numbers into good rolls. Lesson learnt and an instructive position.
