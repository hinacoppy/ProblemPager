(CBp-28)   More on Back Games
pips Red=196 White=103
(CorrectAnswer= 0/0, ErrorScore= 0)
Before he rolled this double four Black had a very well-timed back game. His plan was to keep the 20-pt for as long as possible, then release it and then play a perfectly timed 1-2 back game.

Does this 44 change things or can he stick to his original game plan? 1-2 back games are notoriously difficult to time because as the opponent bears in his checkers quite often he cannot play fives or sixes and this often slows him up considerably.

The rule of thumb is that to successfully time a back game you need to trail by a number of pips equal to the value of the two back game anchors. That means for a 1-2 back game you need to trail by a minimum of 94 pips.

Black was doing all right before this roll but now he will trail by only 77 pips which is not enough to play a 1-2 back game. The likelihood is that his own home board will crash before he hits a shot and it will be impossible to contain any hit checker(s).

Over the board Black tried 14/6, 8/4, 7/3 but that ugly third checker on his 4-pt should have warned him that he was embarking upon the wrong plan. He should have abandoned his ideas for a 1-2 back game and reverted to a 2-5 back game. Admittedly that is not as good as a 1-2 back game but a well-timed 2-5 game is to be much preferred over a busted 1-2 game.

He should have played 24/20(2), 14/6 or 24/20(2), 14/10, 7/3. That way, unless he rolls another couple of small doubles his timing should be perfectly OK for his 2-5 back game. Another point to note is that the 2-5 back game loses fewer gammons than the 1-2 back game because it is easier to run the checkers home.

XG rates 14/6, 8/4, 7/3 as an error (just) and so it is not the worst play ever seen by any means but the key here is to understand when to switch game plans. A big double when playing a back game often predicates a change of plan, in many cases the front anchor has to be abandoned and a simple holding game played instead. In exchange some gammons are saved.
